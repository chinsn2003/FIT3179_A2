Global Forest Change, 2010–2020

Overview of forest extent, spatial loss rates, temporal drivers, and net change.

What is the World Made Of?

Forest Composition

About 31% (~4 Gha) of the globe was covered by forest. 8.5% of that forest was classified as primary forest.

From 2010 to 2020, global forest area declined by ~0.4%, indicating a slow but persistent net loss over the decade.

Overview of Tree Cover Loss

How is Our Primary Forest Changing from 2010–2020?

Annual Loss vs Remaining

Annual loss fluctuates between 3–4 Mha, peaking in 2016 (~6.1 Mha), remaining elevated in 2017 (~5.0 Mha), then easing to ~3.6–3.7 Mha in 2018–2019.

The share of primary forest remaining declines from ~100% in 2010 to ~96.3% in 2020—a reduction of about 3.7 percentage points over the decade.

Loss or Gain?

Deforestation vs Reforestation

Top ten countries by gross forest loss are led by Russia and Brazil, with Canada and the United States following. Most countries show reforestation activity far smaller than their deforestation, indicating net losses overall.

Loss caused by different drivers over time

Loss rises through the mid-2010s and peaks in 2016–2017, driven mainly by permanent agriculture and logging, with a noticeable wildfire surge. Shifting cultivation remains moderate, while settlements & infrastructure, hard commodities, and other natural disturbances contribute smaller, persistent shares.

Who leads overall — and by driver?

Across most years, Russia, Brazil, and Canada account for the largest share of global tree-cover loss. However, the leaders shift when loss is broken down by specific drivers:

  • Permanent agriculture: Brazil
  • Wildfire: Russia, Canada
  • Logging: United States
  • Shifting cultivation: Democratic Republic of the Congo
  • Hard commodities: Indonesia, Russia
  • Settlements & infrastructure: United States
